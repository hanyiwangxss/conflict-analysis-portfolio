Introduction

The omnipresent adoption of AI-driven tools nowadays not only stimulated this research by AI-assisted coding, but has also fundamentally reshaped the macro-level patterns of global warfare. While scholars have cautioned against consequences of AI-augmented autonomous weapon systems (AWS) on nuclear security and strategic escalation (Johnson, 2020), a more immediate and tangible impact exist in the normalization of drone strikes in contemporary conflicts. Extensive analysis suggests that 2024 and 2025 witnessed a significant rise in drone use in wars compared with four or five years ago, when many of these conflicts were just in the beginning stages (Mon, 2025; Santora et al., 2025). Despite the widespread usage of drone technology, the differentiated level of technological development and adoption across nations may result in varied attack patterns. This preliminary research thus aims to investigate the potential relationship between a nation's AI development and its drone strike modalities. As two key conflict zones in 2025, Ukraine and Myanmar are selected as the comparison group, given the significant sample size of air/drone attacks and the potential gap in their respective AI capabilities.

Methodology and Data Preparation

This research primarily utilizes regionally aggregated data from the Armed Conflict Location & Event Data Project (ACLED) to delineate air and drone strike patterns in Ukraine and Myanmar throughout 2025. By filtering the dataset specifically for the year 2025 and the "Air/drone strike" sub-event type, relevant data were extracted from the ACLED Aggregated Data for Europe and Central Asia, as well as the Asia-Pacific region. To ensure quantitative rigor in measuring AI development, this study introduces a second dataset: the Government AI Readiness Index 2025, published by Oxford Insights. This index evaluates each country's AI readiness across six primary pillars and several sub-pillars. The analytical focus involves visualizing drone attack frequencies and their temporal evolution throughout 2025 in both target countries. By calculating the fatality rate per strike, this research further evaluates the lethality and tactical impact of drone operations in each context. Subsequently, by incorporating the scores of each AI readiness pillar, the study first compares the capabilities gap between the two nations and then employs an elasticity analysis to determine how drone strike patterns respond to variances in specific AI readiness sub-pillars. Through this approach, the research seeks to explore the preliminary correlations between varying levels of AI development and their manifestation in modern drone warfare.

📊 1. Drone Strike Overview & Monthly Trends

📝 Analysis

In terms of total event count, Ukraine demonstrated an absolute dominance compared to Myanmar, with its strike volume reaching almost nine times that of Myanmar. The normalized high frequency and large scale of drone attacks suggest that drones have become attritable systems—low-cost and high-volume assets used to overwhelm enemy defenses. This state-on-state conflict has likely led to the industrialization of drone production and adoption. Meanwhile, analysis suggests that in the internal conflict in Myanmar between the military and the resistance, the use of many self-produced or repurposed agricultural drones has resulted in a lower frequency of strikes with a more limited impact scale per event.

The monthly analysis also shows that Ukraine witnessed fluctuations in strike events, while Myanmar demonstrated a steady monthly trend at a low level. Reports show that in the Russia-Ukraine conflict, both nations aimed to produce three to four million drones in 2025. This scaled and industrialized production has led to a "scale effect" and a highly organized attacking mode. For instance, May and September 2025 could have been strategic attacking windows, where explosive cluster strikes occurred under the advantages of AI algorithms in perception, decision, and action (Husain, 2021).

🤖 3. AI Readiness Comparison

📝 Analysis

The divergence in drone strikes demonstrated before is more self-explanatory in regard to the gaps shown in the AI Readiness Index. Ukraine's overall score is almost three times that of Myanmar, which could be a basis to support the scaled drone production and usage in conflicts. Delving into the six main pillars, while regulation, governance, and the socio-economic impacts of AI algorithms are highly uncontrollable in the real battlefield, the AI Infrastructure and Development & Diffusion section are worth attention. Still, when we leave aside the policy and governance section, we can have a preliminary glance at the huge gaps between Ukraine and Myanmar in regard to these pillars. When it comes to AI Infrastructure and Development & Diffusion, the inter-nation differences are narrower.

🔍 4. Sub-Pillar Detailed Analysis

📝 Analysis

This section provides a detailed comparison of AI sub-pillars. Overall, Myanmar demonstrates a significantly lower rating, with all sub-pillars scoring below 40, whereas Ukraine's scores are consistently higher. However, by ordering the pillars by descending differences, we find that Compute Capacity, Enabling Technical Infrastructure, and AI Sector Maturity show the smallest gaps between the two nations.

Compared to the massive disparities in Regulatory Compliance or e-Government Delivery, these material pillars show a relatively narrowed gap. This suggests that the basic material capacity required to operate drones is increasingly accessible even in low-readiness contexts. Nevertheless, the persistent deficiency in Human Capital and Governance Principles in Myanmar explains why its drone usage remains tactical and unorganized, failing to reach the industrialized scale effect witnessed in Ukraine.

⚠️ 5. Strike Impact: Fatalities

📝 Analysis: Strike Impact and Lethality Patterns

The final dimension of this comparison examines the human cost and tactical efficiency of drone operations in both regions. Data from the left reveals a striking inverse relationship between strike frequency and lethality per event.

Ukraine's Model of High-Volume & Low-Lethality: While Ukraine conducted a massive number of strikes (over 29,000), its Fatalities per Strike remain remarkably low at 0.16. This suggests that in a high-AI-readiness context, drones are not only used for direct kinetic elimination, but can also serve broader tactical purposes, such as psychological suppression, or electronic warfare. The objective appears to be overwhelming the enemy's defense systems through sheer volume rather than maximizing casualties in every single event.

Myanmar's Model of Low-Volume & High-Lethality: In contrast, Myanmar's drone operations exhibit a much higher lethality rate of 0.79 fatalities per strike, nearly five times that of Ukraine. Given the lower frequency of attacks (3,193), this indicates a high-value targeting strategy. Without the capacity for industrialized, mass-scale drone swarms, the resistance and military in Myanmar likely reserve their limited drone assets for high-impact, precision strikes on personnel clusters, where each event is intended to achieve maximum lethal effect.

This disparity reinforces the previous sub-pillar analysis. Ukraine's superior Governance and Infrastructure allow for an industrialized attrition war, where drones are used as expendable tools of mass pressure. Meanwhile, Myanmar's reliance on repurposed or agricultural drones, as reflected in its lower AI Sector Maturity, necessitates a more cautious, lethal-oriented deployment to compensate for the lack of technical scale.

📉 6. Elasticity Analysis

📝 Analysis

The final layer of this research employs an Elasticity Analysis to measure the sensitivity of drone strike frequency to different AI readiness pillars. By calculating the elasticity proxy of (log Strike ratio / log Readiness ratio), we can identify which technical factors act as the most resilient catalysts for scaled drone warfare. There are three key patterns:

The Dominance of Technical Infrastructure: The top bar chart reveals that Enabling Technical Infrastructure has the highest elasticity over 12. This indicates that for every marginal gain in foundational technical capacity, the frequency of drone strikes increases exponentially. This reinforces the "Hyperwar" theory, suggesting that once a basic technical threshold is met, the scale of autonomous conflict can expand rapidly (Husain, 2021).

Social and Human Capital as Multipliers: Societal Transition and Human Capital also show high elasticity (around 5). This suggests that a nation's ability to integrate AI into its social fabric and its pool of skilled personnel are significant force multipliers. In Ukraine's case, the high Human Capital score translates into a higher performance on drone deployments per AI readiness point compared to Myanmar.

A Choice of Efficiency vs. Scale: Ukraine's strike intensity per AI-score point is significantly higher across almost all pillars, particularly in Compute Capacity. This suggests that Ukraine was not just striking more because it has more AI; it is striking more efficiently with the AI resources it possesses.

The elasticity analysis proves that AI readiness does not influence warfare in a linear model. Instead, Enabling Technical Infrastructure acts as a fundamental pillar. For countries like Ukraine, which have crossed this threshold, the resulting drone strike intensity is disproportionately high. In contrast, for Myanmar, the lower elasticity across pillars suggests that even modest improvements in AI readiness may not immediately lead to a scale effect in Ukraine until the core infrastructure and human capital gaps are bridged.

📝 Discussion and Limitation

The divergence in drone strike patterns is fundamentally driven by AI Readiness. Ukraine's Hyperwar model uses high-frequency strikes supported by superior Governance and Infrastructure, treating drones as industrialized attritable systems. Conversely, Myanmar's high-lethality & low-frequency pattern reflects a tactical reliance on repurposed drones due to lower AI Sector Maturity. Elasticity analysis confirms that Enabling Technical Infrastructure acts as a critical catalyst for exponential conflict expansion. Ultimately, AI readiness determines a nation's transition from improvised drone use to algorithmically coordinated warfare.

The ACLED sub_event_type for "Air/drone strike" includes traditional manned aircraft, which could introduce noise from non-AI-related kinetic events. Furthermore, the dataset aggregates simple remotely-piloted vehicles (RPVs) with advanced autonomous weapon systems (AWS) without distinguishing autonomy levels. These limitations may require further specific research and enhancement.
